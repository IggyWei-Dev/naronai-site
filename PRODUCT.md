# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Aspirational Nigerian women, primarily 25–45, treating luxury hair as a deliberate investment in self-presentation and social presence. They arrive on the site already decided that they want something premium — the site's job is to meet that expectation and make the decision feel earned. They shop with intentionality, not impulse. Mobile is common but desktop is the primary purchase surface. They expect a buying experience that matches the quality of the product.

## Product Purpose

Naronai sells luxury human hair wigs and beauty products in the Nigerian market. The brand's promise — "Leave an impression" — means every purchase is about entering the room changed: commanding presence, being seen, being remembered. Success looks like a woman who feels the site understands her ambition, trusts the product's quality, and completes a purchase that feels like a ceremony rather than a transaction.

## Brand Personality

Bold. Radiant. Unapologetic.

Voice is confident without being cold, celebratory without being loud. Naronai speaks like the most put-together woman in the room — she doesn't need your approval but she will make you look twice. Nigerian womanhood is the cultural anchor; the aesthetic is globally refined but rooted here.

## Anti-references

- **Generic Shopify beauty DTC**: avoid the standard playbook — soft-pink backgrounds, stock-photo hero shots, "SHOP NOW" everywhere, UGC grids as the primary proof. That is not Naronai.
- **Instagram/TikTok-forward brands**: the site needs permanence. It should feel like an atelier, not a feed. Avoid the reel-native aesthetic — motion for motion's sake, ephemeral layout, social-proof overload.
- **Hair extension wholesalers**: no catalogue feel. No bulk pricing cues, no spec-grid product listings without editorial context. Every product deserves a stage.
- **Cold Western luxury (Chanel/YSL mode)**: severe Eurocentric minimalism — white walls, black serif, clinical spacing — erases the brand's cultural identity. Warmth and richness belong here.

## Design Principles

1. **The site itself leaves an impression.** Brand promise applies to every screen. Someone who lands on the homepage should feel something before they read a word. Presence over polish-as-default.
2. **Fewer things, more weight.** An aspirational customer is not browsing randomly. Show less, make each thing matter more. Resist the instinct to fill space with products.
3. **Ceremony in commerce.** The purchase flow is part of the brand. Cart, checkout, confirmation — each should feel like an intentional ritual, not a utility screen bolted onto a beautiful front page.
4. **Unapologetically rooted.** The warmth, richness, and boldness of the palette are cultural, not decorative. Nigerian womanhood is not a niche — it is the brand's entire identity. Design choices that feel "too much" by Western editorial standards are probably exactly right here.
5. **Editorial over promotional.** Copy and layout describe what a piece *does to the woman who wears it*, not what it *is*. Specs are supporting material, not the story.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA minimum. Desktop is the primary purchase surface; responsive mobile is required but not the driving constraint. Reduce motion alternatives required for all animations. Ensure text contrast meets AA against all background surfaces — the warm palette creates some borderline situations to watch.
